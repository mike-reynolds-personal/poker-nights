How does it work?

How poker hands are evaluated

In simple terms, the winning hand is initially determined by a set of 10 ranks. Take a look at the run-down of poker hands for a visual list.

Things become a bit more complicated when two players end up with the same 'hand', for example, both have a pair, two pairs, a flush etc.

The first way a tie is broken is by the face value of the cards - a pair or trip tens would always beat sixes.

If the face value of two player's cards are the same, then the winning hand is decided by one or more 'kickers' - the remaining cards a player can use to make their hand up to the maximum of 5 cards. So for example, when a player has two pairs (four cards) then one kicker is used. In the case of the player having a single pair, up to three kickers are used. These kickers are also judged on their face value, so the highest kicker breaks the tie.

To further complicate this, if the highest kicker for both players is actually a community card (therefore shared by both players), then the evaluation moves to the next highest card available and so on, up to the maximum of five cards taken from the table and those held by the player.

You will see from the poker hands that some hands already consist of five cards (everything from a straight upwards). If the number of cards in the rank permits it, then the kicker rules still apply, so if two players have a Flush, the player with the highest face value in the Flush takes the pot. In the case of two pairs, then the highest pair is used, unless the players share the highest pair, at which point the tie is broken by the second pair - you get the idea!

Straight Flush and Royal Flush don't follow the 'kicker' rules as it is only possible to have one of these hands within a round.

Don't worry too much about remembering all of the ranks and rules if you've just started playing; within a game at Poker Rooms Now the poker hands list is easily accessible from the player menu, so you can always double-check without having to ask you friends 'Is a Full House good or bad?!'

The other big advantage is that the software always calculates the winning hand or when its actually a draw, and therefore a split-pot, along with any side-pot calculations. To learn more about side-pots, then check out our Side-Pot Calculator
